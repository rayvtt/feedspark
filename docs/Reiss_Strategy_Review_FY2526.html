What the archive confirms

Keyword optimisation is a reliable lever in GB and DE (roughly two-thirds of tests both-positive, averaging +5.9%/+6.8%). Title-level phrasing is the opposite story so far: the Dentsu structure, the Gender-position swap and both AI Search Intent tests have all lost to their control — three separate title-phrasing experiments, three losses. The current MASK format has earned that position, not just kept it by default.

Where it's weakest — EU market

EU is the only market where keyword tests trend negative (2 of 6 positive, avg −4.6%/−5.7%), the opposite of GB and DE. Worth reading alongside chapter 13's Local Language case study — a market running English-sourced keywords against non-English search behaviour is exactly the kind of gap that case study is built to catch, though the archive alone doesn't say that's the cause here.

External benchmark — apparel/fashion: a controlled A/B test comparing enriched meta-data vs. standard feeds in Google Merchant Center showed +7.6% CTR lift, +6.3% ROAS growth and 16.4× ROI on enrichment (Stylitics/Tinuiti). Reiss' own 2-year keyword-test average (+5.9% impressions, +6.8% clicks in GB/DE) sits in the same range — the external pattern holds for this account, with EU the clear exception.

Golden Record cross-check

The last audit put Golden Record completeness at 88.6%. Core structural attributes (colour, material, size, gender, age group, GTIN, item-group ID, condition) are 100% filled in this export — the gap sits in the softer content layer above: keywords, description length and highlights, not raw data presence.

Confirmed at full catalogue scale (chapter 08): FeedHero's own live audit of GB/DE/FR — 179,646 real SKUs, not this 4,999-row sample — flags "No keywords" as the single biggest issue in every market (89–97%) and product highlights as a Red-priority gap in DE specifically (74% insufficient). Two independent methods, the same two weak points. The DE/FR content gap GB doesn't have

Six issues sit at or near 100% Red in DE and FR that don't appear on GB's own Red list at all: no lifestyle image, products not human-reviewed, no enhanced data fields, missing optional data fields (all 100% in both DE and FR), plus gender missing from title (DE 85%, FR 87%). Reads as one story — the content-enhancement work behind GB's catalogue hasn't been run on the DE/FR translations at all, not a smaller version of the same gap.

Why this matters for chapter 13

This is the concrete, catalogue-level version of the EU keyword-test underperformance flagged in chapter 05 (33% positive vs. 75% in GB) and the reason the Local Language case study exists. Three independent signals — a task-log gap, an underperforming A/B test, and now a full-catalogue content audit — all pointing at the same non-GB markets.

Market-specific

What's unique to each market

Not every Red flag is shared — some are specific enough to be a targeted fix rather than a structural one.

GB — image quality

99% images not square, 98% scene images (not on plain background), 36% missing required data fields. GB's images are live at 100% coverage (chapter 06) but the format itself needs work — not present as a Red flag in DE or FR.

DE — highlights & trash

74% insufficient product highlights — confirms the 17% average fill rate from chapter 06 at full catalogue scale. 35% flagged as trash products, the highest of the three markets.

FR — category depth

100% of products have a shallow category tree — the widest single gap in this audit. 26% long-materials issue and a 9% duplicate-titles rate (chapter 07) also unique to FR.

Reading three sources together, not picking one: the feed-sample analysis (chapter 06) says what's structurally present on a snapshot; the A/B archive (chapter 05) says what's been tested and proven; this audit says what FeedHero's own rules catch across the full live catalogue today. Where they overlap (keywords, highlights, Item Group ID), that's the strongest possible signal. Where they diverge (title de-duplication: fixed in GB, still 9% in FR) — that's the useful finding, not a contradiction to resolve away.

Zero-click is accelerating

AI overviews and agentic assistants resolve queries without a click. Reiss is either in that answer, or it doesn't exist for that shopper.

The model picks the product

LLMs read structured feeds, attributes and reviews. Titles averaging 53 characters and 42% highlight coverage mean the model has less to work with than the catalogue could give it.

Fashion is answer-shaped

Fit, occasion, fabric, size availability — every purchase decision is a question about a specific SKU in a specific size, and that's exactly what these surfaces answer.

Agentic commerce

Three protocols that let AI agents shop on behalf of consumers

Chapter ten

Spark AI Engine (Tachyon)

Intent generation, description enrichment, visual attribute harvest.

01

Feed extraction

Tachyon reads live Reiss product data — title, description, colour, material, size, price.

02

Intent generation

LLM generates 20–40 long-tail intent phrases per SKU from the real attributes already in the feed.

03

Scoring & validation

Terms scored against search-term reports; winners selected per SKU.

04

Feed injection

Winning terms embedded in title, description, product highlights — closing the 42% highlight gap from chapter 06.

Reiss example — Stanton Petite Wide-Leg Trousers

Input (real SKU from the export): Cream · Cotton · Petite · Size 4 · £58 sale

AI-generated intent terms:
"petite wide-leg trousers for short women"
"cream corduroy trousers office wear"
"cotton wide-leg trousers under £60"
"petite fit trousers 5ft 3in and under"
// 20-40 generated, top 4-6 used per SKU

Why this matters for a 4-brand, 29-market catalogue

Manually writing intent-rich copy for ~5,000 SKUs across 6 brand lines and 29 markets isn't a resourcing question, it's a throughput question. Tachyon generates the long-tail language at catalogue scale from data that's already 100%-complete on the structural side (colour, material, size).

The title-fix precedent (chapter 05) already proved the AOT pipeline can touch every SKU — Tachyon extends the same reach to descriptions and highlights.

One stack, six brand lines

The feed audit didn't find a new strategy to pursue; it found exactly how far the current one has reached, and where it hasn't yet.
